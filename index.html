Caring for Fancy Mice

Mice are easy to care for and make incredibly rewarding companions.

This care guide is an accumulation of facts and tips from various resources including the Animal Humane Society, Petfinder, The Spruce Pets, VetBabble, RSPCA, and personal experience.

Do your research and know what you’re getting yourself into before bringing your furry friend—or friends—home!

General Care

Here's a few mouse facts right off the bat:

  • Their lifespan is 2-3 years
  • They grow to 5-7 inches in adulthood
  • They are good pets for beginners
Their short lifespan, small size, and ease of care make them great pets for children and adults alike.

Typically, you will want to keep either a group of females or a single male. Male mice tend to be aggressive and territorial and may fight with other males. Females, on the other hand, do well in groups and can be quite social. Never keep a male with a female, as they are quick to breed.

Regardless of their sex or how many you have, there are daily and weekly chores you should keep up on.

Daily Care:

  • Clean their food dish
  • Provide fresh food and water
  • Spot clean any quickly dirtying areas (running wheels, for example, tend to dirty quickly)

Weekly Care

  • Clean their housing
  • Change their bedding

Don’t forget to play with your mouse or mice often, as they are very social animals and can get lonely or bored.

Housing

There are a few housing options to consider: glass tanks, wire cages, or DIY plastic bins. You can read about the pros and cons of each below. Regardless of which you choose, there are a few things to remember.

Make sure your mice have plenty of space. A 10 gallon tank can hold up to 4 mice.

Have several inches of bedding. You can use either paper bedding or wood bedding. Never use pine or cedar, as their particles can cause respiratory issues. Aspen is a good choice for a wood bedding.

Soft nesting material should also be provided. Unscented tissues or soft paper towels work well.

Keep their habitat away from direct sunlight and out of reach of other pets like cats and dogs.

Glass Tank

You can use either an aquarium tank, or a reptile tank. Reptile tanks are more expensive, but they open from the front which can make handling easier.

Pros:

  • Easy to clean
  • Secure

Cons:

  • Limited ventilation
  • Heavy weight
  • Top access only (unless using reptile tank)
  • On the expensive side

Wire Cage

If you use a metal cage, such as a bird cage, you need one with small bar spacing. 1/4 inch is the absolute maximum. Keep in mind that smaller mice, including the young, can escape from bar spacing as little as 1/8 inch.

You will also need to make sure the cage has a solid bottom and not a grated one.

Pros:

  • Good ventilation
  • Light weight
  • Bars provide climbing fun

Cons:

  • Higher chance of escape
  • Messier, as bedding is often kicked through the bars
  • Difficult to find 1/4 inch or smaller bar spacing

DIY Plastic Bin

You can convert a plastic storage bin into a mouse habitat easily. There are plenty of videos on YouTube that can teach you how.

Pros:

  • Inexpensive
  • Highly customizable

Cons:

  • Limited ventilation
  • Plastic may retain odor
  • Takes time and skill to make

Avoid small, plastic hamster homes. Though they can be tempting, they are often too small and poorly ventilated.
