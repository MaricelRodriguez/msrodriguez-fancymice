Caring for Fancy Mice

Mice are easy to care for and make incredibly rewarding companions.

This care guide is an accumulation of facts and tips from various resources including the Animal Humane Society, Petfinder, The Spruce Pets, VetBabble, RSPCA, and personal experience.

Do your research and know what you’re getting yourself into before bringing your furry friend—or friends—home!

General Care

Here's a few mouse facts right off the bat:

  • Their lifespan is 2-3 years
  • They grow to 5-7 inches in adulthood
  • They are good pets for beginners
Their short lifespan, small size, and ease of care make them great pets for children and adults alike.

Typically, you will want to keep either a group of females or a single male. Male mice tend to be aggressive and territorial and may fight with other males. Females, on the other hand, do well in groups and can be quite social. Never keep a male with a female, as they are quick to breed.

Regardless of their sex or how many you have, there are daily and weekly chores you should keep up on.

Daily Care:

  • Clean their food dish
  • Provide fresh food and water
  • Spot clean any quickly dirtying areas (running wheels, for example, tend to dirty quickly)

Weekly Care

  • Clean their housing
  • Change their bedding

Don’t forget to play with your mouse or mice often, as they are very social animals and can get lonely or bored.

Housing

There are a few housing options to consider: glass tanks, wire cages, or DIY plastic bins. You can read about the pros and cons of each below. Regardless of which you choose, there are a few things to remember.

Make sure your mice have plenty of space. A 10 gallon tank can hold up to 4 mice.

Have several inches of bedding. You can use either paper bedding or wood bedding. Never use pine or cedar, as their particles can cause respiratory issues. Aspen is a good choice for a wood bedding.

Soft nesting material should also be provided. Unscented tissues or soft paper towels work well.

Keep their habitat away from direct sunlight and out of reach of other pets like cats and dogs.

Glass Tank

You can use either an aquarium tank, or a reptile tank. Reptile tanks are more expensive, but they open from the front which can make handling easier.

Pros:

  • Easy to clean
  • Secure

Cons:

  • Limited ventilation
  • Heavy weight
  • Top access only (unless using reptile tank)
  • On the expensive side

Wire-Top Cage — Jorge Barrios, wikimedia

Wire Cage

If you use a metal cage, such as a bird cage, you need one with small bar spacing. 1/4 inch is the absolute maximum. Keep in mind that smaller mice, including the young, can escape from bar spacing as little as 1/8 inch.

You will also need to make sure the cage has a solid bottom and not a grated one.

Pros:

  • Good ventilation
  • Light weight
  • Bars provide climbing fun

Cons:

  • Higher chance of escape
  • Messier, as bedding is often kicked through the bars
  • Difficult to find 1/4 inch or smaller bar spacing

DIY Plastic Bin

You can convert a plastic storage bin into a mouse habitat easily. There are plenty of videos on YouTube that can teach you how.

Pros:

  • Inexpensive
  • Highly customizable

Cons:

  • Limited ventilation
  • Plastic may retain odor
  • Takes time and skill to make

Avoid small, plastic hamster homes. Though they can be tempting, they are often too small and poorly ventilated.

Diet

Mice need fresh food and water daily. Generally, they should be given generic rodent food which can be bought at most pet stores in pellet or block form.

Their diet should be supplemented with fresh fruits and vegetables. In addition, they can be given seeds, grains, and even the occasional peanut butter as treats.

Rodent Food

Everyday

The generic rodent food should contain at least 16% protein, 18% fiber, and have no more than 4% fat.

Fresh Fruit & Vegetables

2-3 Times a Week

Fresh fruits & vegetables should only be given in small, bite-sized amounts.

Example fruits & vegetables to give include:

  • Peas
  • Bananas
  • Carrots
  • Apples
  • Broccoli
  • Zucchini
  • Cucumber

If you’re not sure about a fruit or vegetable, always look it up before giving it to your mouse.

Seeds & Grains

Occasional Treat

You can find mixed seeds at most pet stores. They should be given seeds and grains only occasionally as treats.

Every mouse is different and they may prefer one type of seed over another. Experiment to see which your mouse likes best!

Peanut butter in the jar — PiccoloNamek, wikimedia

Peanut Butter

Very Occasional Treat

Mice love peanut butter. It can be used as a very occasional treat.

Only give very small amounts. Not only is it an unhealthy snack, but it can also stick in their mouths if they take too much at once. Keep in mind that peanut butter is essentially junk food for mice.

The founder of the Fancy Mice Breeders forum, Dom Langowski, has posted a masterlist of foods that mice can and can’t have.

Hides, Chews & Toys

Mice like to nest, therefore they need at least one “hide” in their habitat. It should be a safe, dark space for them to sleep in, or hide in when they get stressed.

The teeth of a mouse never stop growing, so they need chews to prevent their teeth from getting too long. You can buy chew toys at almost any pet store, or you can provide them with wood that is unpainted and untreated.

Mice need toys for both exercise and fun. Provide them with a running wheel or running saucer. Don’t give them a wheel that’s too small… the wheel should be large enough for your mouse to run with its back and tail straight.

Other fun toys include things to climb on or run through: ladders, playhouses, and tunnels work well. You can even make toys at home with paper towel or toilet paper rolls!

Handling

You’ll need to earn your mouse’s trust in order to handle it. Make sure your mouse has enough time to get comfortable to its new home before beginning the process.

Start by talking to your mouse and spending time by their habitat each day. Give it treats and let it get used to your voice and smell.

Your mouse may begin crawling into your hand on its own, or you can try gently scooping it in your hand.

Remember to be patient! You’re a lot bigger than your little mouse and it may take time for you to earn its trust!

Our Mission
